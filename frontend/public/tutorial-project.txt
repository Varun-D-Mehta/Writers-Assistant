[STORY BIBLE]

[OVERVIEW]
  Title: The Clockmaker's Daughter
  Genre: Fantasy / Mystery
  Setting: Victorian-era London with hidden magical districts
  Time Period: 1883
  Point of View: Third person limited
  Tone & Style: Atmospheric, lyrical, with undercurrents of suspense
  Synopsis: When master clockmaker Elias Thorne dies under mysterious circumstances, his estranged daughter Ada returns to London to claim his workshop — only to discover that her father's clocks don't just tell time. They hold memories, secrets, and doorways to places that shouldn't exist.

[CHARACTERS]
  Character: Ada Thorne
    Description: A 26-year-old woman with ink-stained fingers and her father's
      sharp eyes. She left London at 18 after a bitter argument with Elias
      and spent eight years restoring antique instruments in Edinburgh.
      Practical and skeptical, she initially dismisses the magical properties
      of her father's clocks as grief-induced imagination.
    Traits: resourceful, stubborn, mechanically gifted, emotionally guarded
    Notes: Key arc: from skeptic to believer. Her mechanical skills are
      the bridge — she understands the clockwork but must learn the magic.

  Character: Silas Blackwood
    Description: A tall, gaunt man who appears to be in his 40s but speaks of
      events from centuries past. He was Elias's closest friend and the only
      other person who knows the true nature of the clocks. Runs an antiquarian
      bookshop that serves as a front for the Horologists' Guild.
    Traits: enigmatic, loyal, melancholic, dry-witted
    Notes: Mentor figure. Knows more than he reveals. His own clock is
      running down — a plot point revealed in Act 3.

  Character: Inspector Clement Marsh
    Description: A Scotland Yard detective assigned to investigate Elias's death.
      Methodical and persistent, he senses something unusual about the case
      but can't articulate what. Follows evidence wherever it leads, even
      into impossible places.
    Traits: analytical, dogged, honest, increasingly unsettled
    Notes: Represents the rational world pushing against the magical one.
      Potential ally or antagonist depending on how Ada handles him.

[EVENTS]
  Event 1: The Death of Elias Thorne
    Description: Elias is found dead in his workshop, slumped over an unfinished
      clock. The coroner rules natural causes, but Silas notices the clock's
      hands are spinning backwards and the workshop smells of ozone — signs
      of a temporal fracture.

  Event 2: Ada's Return to London
    Description: Ada arrives at Paddington Station on a grey November morning.
      She hasn't been to London in eight years. The solicitor's letter said
      only that her father left everything to her. She expects a dusty workshop
      and painful memories. She gets both, plus something she never expected.

  Event 3: The First Clock Speaks
    Description: While cataloguing her father's inventory, Ada winds a small
      brass carriage clock. Instead of ticking, it plays a memory — her
      father's voice, speaking words he said the day she left. Ada drops the
      clock. It doesn't break. It keeps playing.

[ENVIRONMENT]
  Setting: Thorne's Clockwork Shop
    Description: A narrow three-story building on Cavendish Row, wedged between
      a tailor and a chemist. The ground floor is the public shop, cluttered
      with grandfather clocks, pocket watches, and maritime chronometers. The
      workshop is in the basement, lit by gas lamps and filled with brass
      gears, crystal pendulums, and tools that have no names in any catalogue.
      The top floor was Elias's living quarters, now covered in dust.
    Climate: Damp London cold seeps through the walls
    Atmosphere: The constant ticking of a hundred clocks creates a living pulse

  Setting: The Horologists' Guild
    Description: Hidden behind an unmarked door in a Clerkenwell alley, the Guild
      occupies a converted chapel. Inside, time behaves strangely — clocks on
      different walls show different hours, and members occasionally flicker
      as if they're not entirely present. The Guild has existed since the 14th
      century, though its records only go back to the Great Fire of 1666,
      when the previous archives were lost (or deliberately destroyed).
    Members: Currently seven, down from a historical peak of thirteen
    Purpose: Maintaining the temporal barriers between London and its echoes

[OBJECTS]
  Object: The Meridian Clock
    Description: An ornate astronomical clock that Elias spent the last three
      years of his life building. It has thirteen faces instead of twelve,
      with the thirteenth showing a location rather than a time. The case is
      made of blackened steel and glass, with internal mechanisms visible —
      gears that seem to move in impossible directions simultaneously.
    Significance: The central MacGuffin. Completing the Meridian Clock is the
      key to sealing the temporal fractures that are spreading across London.
    Notes: Currently incomplete. The final component — a crystal oscillator
      tuned to a specific frequency — was not among Elias's possessions.
      Ada must find or create it.

[MANUSCRIPT]

[PART: Act One — The Inheritance]

[CHAPTER: The Letter]

The letter arrived on a Tuesday, which Ada would later consider appropriate. Tuesdays were her father's favourite day — the day he claimed the week's machinery truly began to turn. Mondays were just the winding, he'd say. Tuesdays were when you heard the first tick.

She read it standing at her workbench in Edinburgh, a half-restored astrolabe pinned under her left hand. The solicitor's handwriting was precise and impersonal: Elias Thorne, deceased. Estate and effects. Her presence requested at the earliest convenience.

Ada set the letter down beside the astrolabe and stared at them both. Two relics from different centuries, equally distant from her present life.

She hadn't spoken to her father in eight years. Eight years since she'd stormed out of the workshop on Cavendish Row, swearing she'd never touch another clock as long as she lived. She'd kept half that promise — she'd left London and never returned. But she couldn't stop working with her hands. The instruments changed. The precision didn't.

[CHAPTER: Cavendish Row]

London smelled the same. That was the first thing Ada noticed stepping off the train at Paddington — the particular blend of coal smoke, horse dung, and river fog that no amount of progress seemed able to scrub away. Edinburgh had its own smell, cleaner and sharper, flavoured with sea salt and brewing hops. London's smell was older, heavier, like something that had been accumulating for centuries.

The cab ride to Cavendish Row took twenty minutes through streets Ada half-remembered. Some landmarks held steady — the British Museum's iron fence, the plane trees along Russell Square — while others had changed entirely. A new department store where a bookshop had been. An electric lamp where gas had burned. The city was editing itself, page by page, and Ada had missed several chapters.

Number 14 Cavendish Row looked smaller than she remembered. The brass plaque still read THORNE & SON — HOROLOGICAL INSTRUMENTS, though there had never been a son. Just a daughter who'd left.

She stood on the pavement with her single trunk and looked up at the dark windows. The shop was closed, of course. Had been since Elias died three weeks ago. The solicitor had arranged for a neighbour to wind the clocks — a detail that struck Ada as both practical and deeply sad. Someone had to keep them alive. Even now, even with their maker gone.
